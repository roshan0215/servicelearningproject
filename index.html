Digital Literacy for Seniors

A Policy Research Project on Bridging the Technology Gap - By Roshan Sathish

Research Overview

The Digital Divide Among Seniors

Digital literacy for seniors is very important and represents a critical policy challenge at this current time. As essential services are now migrating online, older adults face barriers including limited technology access, inadequate digital skills, and increased vulnerability to cybersecurity threats and financial scams.

Key Findings

Research shows that seniors are disproportionately targeted by online fraud, and they lose billions annually. They also experience social isolation that could be addressed through digital connectivity.

Effective Policy Interventions

Effective policy interventions include digital literacy training programs, cybersecurity education, and community-based support that have shown significant improvements in seniors' health outcomes, social connections, and ability to safely navigate online.

The Path Forward

Addressing this digital divide requires a comprehensive approach that combines education, accessibility improvements, and protective measures to ensure seniors can fully participate in an increasingly digital society.

Academic Integration

The policy issue of digital literacy for seniors does intersect with several units in AP Government and Politics, specifically Unit 1.7 with Relationship Between States and the Federal Government, Unit 2.12 with the Bureaucracy, and Unit 4.8 with Ideology and Policy Making.

Unit 1.7: Relationship Between States and the Federal Government

Digital Literacy for seniors shows the relationship between federal and state governments in addressing policy challenges. The Massachusetts Executive Office of Elder Affairs' $1.5 million grant program, funded through the federal government's American Rescue Plan Act, demonstrates cooperative federalism. While the federal government provided funding and broad policy direction, Massachusetts designed and implemented the specific program parameters, allowing local Councils on Aging to distribute up to $100,000 per municipality for technology purchases and training programs.

This arrangement reflects the federal government's ability to address national issues while still respecting the states and their autonomy to let them implement it their way. In this scenario, the federal government recognized digital literacy as a national priority affecting vulnerable populations, but instead of creating a uniform national program, it gave the resources to the states to let them adapt to their specific needs and structures.

Unit 2.12: The Bureaucracy

The federal response to digital literacy challenges demonstrates the state's role in policy implementation and service delivery. CISA's cybersecurity awareness program for older Americans shows how federal agencies can use their authority in interpreting broad mandates. Operating under the Department of Homeland Security, CISA has expanded its infrastructure to include vulnerable populations, showing how bureaucratic agencies can adapt to emerging challenges.

The agency's resources contain information from the FBI on fraud prevention, the FTC on identity protection, and the FCC on smartphone security, and demonstrates the bureaucracy's function in policy areas that span multiple jurisdictions. The implementation of these digital literacy programs also demonstrates the challenges of accountability and performance measurement. Programs like the Senior Vitality program at UCB's Curry Senior Center provide evidence-based models for bureaucratic effectiveness. However, scaling such programs nationally requires federal agencies to make them relatively standardized, while also allowing it to have some local adaptation.

Unit 4.8: Ideology and Policy Making

Digital literacy policy for seniors reveals how ideological perspectives can impact policy approaches and priorities. Conservatives emphasize individual responsibility and market-based solutions, which is more favorable to private-sector digital literacy training. Meanwhile, liberal ideology is more focused around the government ensuring equal access to services and funding these programs.

The policy challenge also intersects with competing values around privacy and security. The Stanford Center on Longevity's research shows that seniors are up to 7x more likely to share misinformation. This shows how it's important to protect both vulnerable populations while also allowing autonomy for individuals. Conservatives might emphasize how they would need to educate themselves somehow, while liberals would focus more on stronger platform regulation and government intervention.

Taking Action

Making a real difference in digital literacy for seniors requires concrete action at the community level. Here are the steps being taken to address this critical issue:

  • Write a letter to a local representative — Advocating for increased funding and support for digital literacy programs at the state and local level.
  • Volunteer at a Non-Profit Assisted Living (Glencroft) — Helping at their marketplace and engaging with residents in the community.
  • Community Outreach Presentations — Visiting multiple retirement homes, assisted living facilities, and 55+ communities to present about phishing and other online scams, equipping seniors with the knowledge to protect themselves.
  • Writing Educational Blogs — Creating online content through Senior Web Warden to share scam awareness tips, digital safety guides, and resources that reach seniors and their families beyond in-person presentations.

Letter to Representative Abraham Hamadeh

Dear Representative Hamadeh,

My name is Roshan Sathish, and I am a senior at Mountain Ridge High School in Glendale, Arizona. For my AP U.S. Government and Politics service learning project, I chose to research and advocate for digital literacy programs for seniors. I have been presenting on phishing and online scams at multiple retirement homes and assisted living facilities across Glendale, Surprise, and Peoria. I am writing to urge you to support increased federal funding for digital literacy training programs that would protect Arizona's vulnerable senior population.

Arizona has the highest rate of elder fraud in the nation at 289 cases per 100,000 seniors. In 2024 alone, over 6,600 Arizona seniors reported losses totaling nearly $191 million. Nationwide, Americans aged 60 and older lost $4.8 billion to scammers in 2024, representing an 83% increase from the previous year. Through my presentations at retirement communities, I have witnessed firsthand how seniors struggle with basic online security. Many do not recognize phishing emails, fall victim to romance scams, and lose their life savings to tech support frauds. As essential services increasingly move online, this digital divide leaves the elderly population isolated, vulnerable, and excluded from vital resources.

Successful models already exist that demonstrate the effectiveness of federal investment in digital literacy. Massachusetts allocated $1.5 million in American Rescue Plan Act funding for digital literacy grants, allowing municipalities to provide technology and training through local Councils on Aging. Federal agencies like CISA have developed cybersecurity awareness programs specifically for older Americans, partnering with the FBI, FTC, and FCC to provide comprehensive resources. These evidence-based programs have demonstrated real results. UC Berkeley's Senior Vitality program showed significant improvements in seniors' health outcomes, social connections, and online safety. District 8, with its large concentration of retirees in Sun City, Surprise, and surrounding communities, would benefit immensely from similar federal support.

I respectfully ask that you support legislation to expand federal funding for digital literacy training programs and support initiatives that bring cybersecurity education to seniors. This addresses not only fraud prevention but also ensures that the elderly population can access healthcare, stay connected with loved ones, and maintain independence in an increasingly digital world. I would welcome the opportunity to share more insights from my community work and discuss how we can better protect Arizona's seniors.

Thank you for your time and consideration. I look forward to your response.

Sincerely,
Roshan Sathish
Mountain Ridge High School, Class of 2026
Glendale, AZ 85310

Reflection

What are some of the pressing needs/issues in the community? How does this project address those needs?

Arizona has the highest rate of elder fraud in the nation at 289 cases per 100,000 seniors, with over 6,600 victims reporting nearly $191 million in losses in 2024 alone. Many seniors lack the digital literacy skills to recognize phishing emails, calls, and text messages. As essential services increasingly move online, this knowledge gap leaves elderly residents isolated and vulnerable to exploitation. My presentations at retirement communities directly address this need by teaching seniors how to identify common scams and protect their personal information online, such as Medicare, utility, and delivery scams.

What learning occurred for you in this experience? How can you apply this learning?

This project deepened my understanding of how federal and state governments work together to address policy challenges through cooperative federalism. I learned that while the federal government can provide funding and broad policy direction through programs like the American Rescue Plan Act, states and local communities must implement solutions that fit their specific needs. Working directly with seniors showed me that policy issues affect people with real consequences. I can apply this understanding of federalism and policy implementation to other social issues that require coordination between different levels of government.

How can you continue your involvement with this group or social issue?

I plan to continue presenting at retirement communities and assisted living facilities throughout the school year to reach more seniors. Additionally, I want to connect with organizations like ROSE (Resources/Outreach to Safeguard the Elderly) that focus on preventing elder fraud in Arizona. I will also continue writing and uploading blog posts on seniorwebwarden.com in order to reach more people and communities and educate them on how to avoid scams.

How can you educate others or raise awareness about this group or social issue?

Beyond my direct presentations to seniors, I can share information about elder fraud prevention with families who may have elderly relatives. I could work with my school or other local communities to organize awareness campaigns or information sessions about protecting seniors from online scams. Social media platforms provide an opportunity to share resources and warning signs that reach a broader audience. I also will start providing retirement homes with flyers with steps on how to avoid and report scams.

What would you like to learn more about, related to this project or issue?

I would like to learn more about the psychological tactics that scammers use to manipulate seniors and how understanding these techniques could improve educational programs. Additionally, I want to explore how other countries address digital literacy for elderly populations and whether their approaches could be adapted for the United States. I am also interested in understanding how technologies like AI-generated deepfakes and voice cloning will evolve in scams so I can educate the seniors on how to avoid them.
